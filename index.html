Introduction to Henna

Henna, also known as mehndi, is a natural dye made from the leaves of the henna plant (Lawsonia inermis). For centuries, it has been used for body art, especially in cultures across South Asia, the Middle East, and North Africa.
Henna designs, which range from intricate and delicate to bold and simple, are traditionally applied to the hands, feet, and other parts of the body. The art of henna is not just cosmetic; it holds deep cultural significance in many communities.
Henna is commonly used during significant events like weddings, festivals, and religious ceremonies. The application process is a ritualistic and joyous occasion, especially in bridal celebrations. The dye is applied in paste form and, once dried, leaves behind a deep orange to reddish-brown stain that lasts for days or even weeks.
With its rich history and cultural importance, henna remains a symbol of beauty, tradition, and celebration, transcending time and borders.
